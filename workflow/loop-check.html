🔄 Loop Check

Loop check is the process of verifying that all instrumentation and control loops are correctly wired, calibrated, and functioning as per the design specifications before commissioning.

🔹 What is a Loop Check?

A loop check is a systematic verification of each control loop from the field instrument (sensor/transmitter) through the control system (PLC/DCS) to the final control element (valve/drive). It ensures that signals are correctly transmitted, processed, and responded to. Loop checks are essential for identifying wiring errors, configuration mistakes, and calibration issues before system startup.

🔹 Types of Loop Checks

  • Analog Loop Check – Verification of 4-20 mA signals from transmitters to PLC/DCS and from controllers to final elements
  • Digital Loop Check – Verification of discrete signals (on/off) from switches, alarms, and interlocks
  • Communication Loop Check – Verification of digital communication protocols (HART, Profibus, Modbus, etc.)
  • Control Loop Check – Verification of PID control response and setpoint tracking

🔹 Key Steps in Loop Checking

  • Preparation – Gather loop drawings, instrument datasheets, and test equipment (multimeter, signal generator, calibrator)
  • Visual Inspection – Check wiring, terminal connections, and labeling against drawings
  • Continuity Check – Verify that wires are properly connected and no open/short circuits exist
  • Signal Injection – Simulate a signal at the transmitter and verify the reading at the PLC/DCS and HMI
  • Response Check – Verify that the final control element (valve/drive) responds correctly to the controller output
  • Alarm & Interlock Check – Test alarms and safety interlocks under simulated conditions
  • Documentation – Record all results and sign off on loop check sheets

🔹 Example: Loop Check for a Pressure Control Loop

A pressure control loop includes a pressure transmitter, a PLC with PID control, and a control valve. The loop check team injects a 4 mA signal (0% pressure) and verifies the reading on the PLC. They then inject 12 mA (50%) and 20 mA (100%) and verify the corresponding readings. They also verify that the control valve opens/closes correctly in response to the PLC output. Alarms and interlocks are tested by simulating high/low pressure conditions.
