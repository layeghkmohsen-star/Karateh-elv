⚖️ Calibration

Procedures, standards, and best practices for calibrating industrial instruments to ensure accuracy and reliability.

🔹 What is Calibration?

Calibration is the process of comparing a measuring instrument's output against a known reference standard. The purpose is to detect and correct any deviation to ensure the instrument provides accurate measurements. Regular calibration is essential for quality control, safety, and regulatory compliance.

🔹 Key Calibration Steps

  • Preparation – Select appropriate reference standard, ensure clean environment
  • Zero Adjustment – Set instrument to read zero with no input
  • Span Adjustment – Set instrument to read the full-scale value
  • Multi-point Verification – Check at 25%, 50%, 75% of range
  • Documentation – Record results, calculate error, and issue calibration certificate

🔹 Common Calibration Standards

  • ISA 51.1 – Terminology for calibration
  • ISO 10012 – Measurement management systems
  • NIST Traceable – Calibration traceable to national standards
  • IEC 61511 – Functional safety calibration requirements

🔹 Example: Pressure Transmitter Calibration

A Rosemount 3051 pressure transmitter (0-100 bar) is calibrated using a deadweight tester. The technician applies known pressures (0, 25, 50, 75, 100 bar) and records the 4-20 mA output. The results are plotted against the ideal curve, and any deviation is adjusted. The calibration certificate is issued with a due date for the next calibration.
