💰 Cost Estimation

Methods, techniques, and tools for estimating project costs, budgeting, cost control, and forecasting in engineering projects.

🔹 What is Cost Estimation?

Cost estimation is the process of forecasting the financial resources required to complete a project. In engineering, accurate cost estimation is essential for budgeting, project approval, procurement, and financial control. It involves analyzing past data, identifying cost drivers, and estimating costs for labor, materials, equipment, and overhead.

🔹 Cost Estimation Methods

  • Analogous Estimation – Based on historical data from similar projects
  • Parametric Estimation – Using statistical models and cost per unit (e.g., cost per meter, per kW)
  • Bottom-Up Estimation – Detailed estimation by breaking down work into components and summing costs
  • Three-Point Estimation – Using optimistic, pessimistic, and most likely scenarios
  • Expert Judgment – Leveraging expertise from experienced professionals
  • Vendor Quotes – Obtaining quotes from suppliers and contractors

🔹 Key Cost Components

  • Direct Costs – Labor, materials, equipment rental, subcontractor costs
  • Indirect Costs – Overhead, administration, utilities, insurance
  • Contingency – Reserve for unforeseen expenses (typically 5–20% of total)
  • Escalation – Allowance for inflation and price increases during project duration
  • Profit Margin – For contractors and service providers

🔹 Example: Cost Estimation for a Substation Project

A cost estimator prepares a budget for a 132 kV substation project. They use parametric estimation based on cost per MVA, bottom-up estimation for equipment (transformers, switchgear), and vendor quotes for specialized items. A 10% contingency is added, and escalation is factored in for a 2-year construction period. The total estimated cost is presented to the client for approval.
